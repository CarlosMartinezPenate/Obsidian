# Introduction

Pollution in the sea is often patchy, short-lived, and chemically complex. Even when surface waters look clear, contaminants may be subsurface, rapidly diluted below analytical detection limits, or missed by physical sensors, whether in situ (e.g., gliders) or remote (e.g., satellites) (Berenshtein et al., 2020). Chemical assays can provide precise measurements, but they frequently miss transient events or contaminants outside targeted detection ranges. Moreover, targeted chemical assays typically require prior knowledge of which pollutant to measure among thousands of possible compounds. These constraints motivate biological indicators that register organismal responses even when contaminants are intermittent, diluted, or outside chemical and physical detectability (Ferreira et al., 2023; Yarsan & Yipe, 2013).

Microbial communities are promising indicators because they turn over rapidly, interact directly with dissolved and particulate matter, and shift in composition on timescales relevant to exposure (Anthony et al., 2023; Fossi et al., 2000). High-throughput 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing—especially at amplicon sequence variant (ASV) resolution—captures these shifts in stable, single-nucleotide units that are reusable across time and datasets, enabling sensitive detection of compositional change (Jurburg, 2025). Pollution events—especially hydrocarbon spills and repeated or chronic contamination—can leave detectable community and functional signatures that outlast direct chemical or physical detection (Bik et al., 2012; George et al., 2025; Potts et al., 2022). Although microbial monitoring still often relies on ship-based sampling, autonomous samplers that preserve nucleic acids in situ are expanding temporal coverage and better matching the intermittency of pollution events (Knapik et al., 2020).

Interpreting any single field sample, however, remains challenging because ASV occurrence and relative abundance also vary with seasonality, shifting water masses, and intrinsic ecological variability. Long-term baselines address this problem by defining statistical normalcy—the expected distribution of community states under typical conditions—so that deviations can be evaluated against a historical range rather than against a single comparator (Banchi et al., 2022; Ji et al., 2024). In this sense, a time-series baseline effectively turns the microbial community into a long-term sensor, enabling baseline-referenced detection of statistically unusual ASV abundance patterns rather than relying on single–time-point differences.

In the Eastern Mediterranean Sea (EMS), a strong winter storm in February 2021 drove weathered oil ashore along ~180 km of Israel’s southeastern Levantine coast. Deposition was highly patchy, and rough seas hindered satellite detection and delayed glider missions (Herut, 2021). The 2021 tar pollution event prompted rapid-response efforts that included coastal campaigns across impacted areas and survey cruises during and after the event. Size-fractionated microbial DNA was sampled across depths along predicted pollution trajectories and from impacted coastal sites.

In oligotrophic seas, microbial responses are often structured by habitat, motivating separate analyses of free-living (FL; 0.22–11 µm) and particle-associated (PA; >11 µm) fractions (Lauro et al., 2009). This fractionation is particularly relevant for petroleum exposure because oil droplets, oil–particle aggregates, and weathered tar residues behave physically as particles and create discrete, colonizable surfaces. Under storm-forced conditions, however, such signals are unlikely to be vertically homogeneous. Instead, particle redistribution, transient aggregation, and trapping near density interfaces can localize biologically relevant exposure into depth-restricted hotspots, such that the strongest microbial response may appear in a particular fraction and depth horizon rather than as a smooth whole-water-column transition (Herut et al., 2024; Tedetti et al., 2013).

A central challenge in candidate microbial indicator discovery is specificity. Oil introduces a complex mixture of hydrocarbons that can function as both substrates and stressors (Head et al., 2006), yet many taxa that increase after oil exposure also bloom during natural pulses of dissolved organic matter (DOM), especially during the release of phytoplankton exudates in seasonal blooms. Opportunistic copiotrophs such as Alteromonadales, Flavobacteriales, and Rhodobacterales can respond strongly to both petroleum-derived and biogenic inputs (Head et al., 2006). As a result, oil-driven responses can be obscured unless non-oil DOM controls are included and field interpretation is anchored to robust baselines that capture background variability (Bagi et al., 2022; Knapik et al., 2020; Roth Rosenberg et al., 2021; Shai et al., 2021).

Here, we ask two linked questions: (1) Can ASV-level responses distinguish oil-derived carbon from generic organic enrichment under controlled conditions? (2) If so, are those responses detectable in spill-context field samples from the 2021 EMS tar event? We used a 48-h microcosm seeded with natural EMS seawater and compared four treatments: weathered tar, pyrene, phytoplankton exudates, and an unamended control. We analyzed PA and FL fractions separately and paired community changes with bacterial production (BP), primary production (PP), and N₂ fixation to probe underlying trophic processes. Candidate microbial indicators were nominated using conservative, multi-method differential-abundance consensus in the microcosms, then contextualized against the Southeastern Mediterranean Monthly Series (SoMMoS; 2018–2019) baseline by testing whether they rose above 99th-quantile, fraction-specific expected ranges. Because the field samples lack paired hydrocarbon chemistry, we treat these signals as candidate indicators rather than chemically confirmed indicators. We further examine these candidate indicators in an independent regional dataset that includes sampling during the 2021 event time frame and interpret them in the context of depth-resolved physical–biogeochemical structure during storm forcing.

# Methodology

## Study design overview

We combined (i) archived, size-fractionated field samples collected during and after the February 2021 EMS tar pollution event with (ii) a controlled 48-h microcosm designed to separate oil-derived amendment effects from generic DOM enrichment. Candidate ASV indicators were nominated from the experiment and then screened in pollution-context field samples by testing whether they were observed above fraction-specific thresholds derived from a long-term regional baseline (SoMMoS). We further explored pollution-event-context samples metadata for candidate microbial indicator depth-resolved distribution patterns. To explore the distribution of candidate microbial indicator abundances in a parallel, independent snapshot, an independent dataset sampled during the 2021 EMS pollution event was used.

## Field sampling and baseline context

Pollution-context samples were collected during the 2021 rapid-response effort and in a 2022 follow-up, spanning offshore-to-nearshore locations and including field blanks. Communities were collected by size-fractionated filtration to resolve particle-associated (PA; >11 µm) and free-living (FL; 0.22–11 µm) fractions. To contextualize event samples, we used the Southeastern Mediterranean Monthly Series (SoMMoS) time series as a baseline, which includes depth-resolved sampling at two EMS marine observatory stations across ~180 samples. Baseline distributions were used to define candidate-specific expected ranges for anomaly screening (see below). In addition, we analyzed an independent, non-size-fractionated 16S rRNA dataset provided by the **IOLR**, collected in parallel during the 2021 EMS pollution event. This independent snapshot was anchored to the **Haifa Transect Time Series** baseline to evaluate the distribution of our candidate microbial indicators across depths.

## Microcosm experiment and treatments

To identify ASVs responding to oil-like, recalcitrant, oil-like, dissolved organic carbon (DOC) vs those ASVs responding to labile, "natural" DOC, a phytoplankton-exudate controlled experiment was designed: surface seawater was collected offshore (Levantine Basin) and incubated for ~48 h under near in situ temperature and a diel light regime. Four treatments were established in biological replicate bottles: weathered tar, pyrene (DMSO carrier), phytoplankton exudates (0.22 µm filtered), and an unamended control. A preliminary tar dose pilot and pyrene axenic culture assays were performed to inform amendment concentrations (Supplementary Methods). Rate measurements (flow cytometry for cell abundance, bacterial production by leucine incorporation, primary production by ^14C assimilation, and N₂ fixation by ^15N₂ uptake) were conducted alongside community sampling.

## DNA sequencing and processing

At T0 and at the incubation endpoint, community DNA was collected by sequential filtration into PA and FL fractions; negative controls (filtration blanks and extraction blanks) were processed alongside samples. 16S rRNA amplicons (V4–V5 region) were sequenced with negative and positive controls. Reads were processed using standard denoising to generate ASVs, taxonomically annotated against SILVA, and analyzed within a phylogenetic/multivariate framework (Supplementary Methods).

## Contamination control and community analyses

We evaluated contamination using available field negative and extraction negative controls and applied a conservative decontamination using decontam (with most p-threshold = 0.5000), emphasizing prevalence-based detection within relevant subsets. Community composition was analyzed with Bray–Curtis dissimilarities and NMDS ordination, and group differences were tested using PERMANOVA with appropriate multiple-testing control.

## Differential abundance and candidate indicator screening

To improve robustness to method-specific artifacts, differential abundance (DA) was assessed using multiple frameworks, and candidates were defined conservatively. Within each fraction, candidates were nominated from the microcosm as ASVs that were treatment-specific and supported by directionally consistent results in ≥2 DA methods, excluding signatures attributable to bottle effects (e.g., seawater T0 vs control). For field screening, each candidate was evaluated against SoMMoS baseline distributions by defining fraction-specific high-quantile thresholds (Q95/Q99) of non-zero relative abundances. Pollution-context samples were flagged when candidate ASVs were observed above the corresponding baseline threshold in the relevant fraction; statistical support relative to baseline was assessed using prevalence- and abundance-based nonparametric tests with false-discovery-rate correction. Because per-sample hydrocarbon chemistry was not available, field signals are interpreted as candidate indicators rather than chemically confirmed indicators.

# Results

## Candidate microbial indicators: incubation nomination, baseline anchoring, and field screening

Candidate microbial indicators were nominated from incubation DA results as ASVs that were (i) enriched with ≥2-method support, (ii) treatment-specific, and (iii) not attributable to bottle effects. This yielded 26 nominated ASVs: 24 in FL (exudate-nominated) and 2 in PA (tar-nominated: ASV67 and ASV384). No pyrene-nominated candidates were identified.

We evaluated these candidates against SoMMoS baseline distributions using fraction-specific Q95/Q99 thresholds derived from non-zero relative abundances. Across pollution-context samples, 10 of 26 candidates were observed above their fraction-specific baseline threshold at least once. Eight candidates rose above the baseline threshold in the same fraction in which they were nominated, whereas the two tar-nominated PA candidates were above the threshold only in the opposite fraction (FL). Among these above-baseline candidates, a subset showed FDR-significant support by Fisher and/or Wilcoxon tests within fraction, and an additional subset was supported by an independent field-vs-baseline DA contrast (Fig. W). Together, these results summarize the pipeline from incubation nomination → baseline screening → statistical support in spill-context samples. To understand how those anomalies were structured in the water column, we next examined their depth distribution across Cruise 2 and an independent regional dataset sampled during the same event window.

## Independent (IOLR) samples show shallow, patchy candidate-ASV anomalies

The MAX (IOLR) tar-cruise dataset provides a second, independent, depth-resolved window into the same event period. In the MAX tar-cruise samples (HZ_2021_02_Tar), biomarker anomalies are confined to a subset of shallow depths and, critically, are not restricted to the tar-linked candidate ASV set. Instead, they include candidate lineages more plausibly interpreted as responders to labile DOM and particle-associated niches.

Two aspects of the MAX anomaly pattern are particularly informative. First, the NS3a marine group candidate ASV448 shows surface-intensified anomalies at 1 m and 3 m, consistent with an upper-ocean envelope of biologically legible disturbance during the response window and with the idea that near-surface waters can carry strong DOM and particle signals even when deeper layers exhibit different dynamics.

Second, the putatively tar-linked _Glaciecola_ ASV67 is observed above the baseline threshold at 15 m and 30 m, but it does not simply track the NS3a signal: ASV67 is above the threshold alone at 15 m, while co-occurring with ASV448 only at 30 m. This non-obligate co-occurrence indicates that the observed field signal is mixed rather than expressed as a single, uniform candidate-ASV assemblage across depths.

# Discussion

## Lab-to-field translation is constrained by missing chemistry and baseline mismatch

Field validation is limited primarily by the absence of per-sample hydrocarbon chemistry in spill-context samples. Consequently, field screening should be interpreted as baseline-referenced anomaly detection rather than confirmation of pollutant causality (Joye et al., 2016). Using SoMMoS as a baseline provides a principled way to define expected ASV abundance ranges and to flag statistically unusual observations, but mismatch remains: SoMMoS stations differ from nearshore impacted habitats, and baseline years (2018–2019) do not fully capture 2021 conditions (Reich et al., 2022). These differences can inflate or deflate the probability that a taxon appears above threshold because of strong coastal or seasonal structure.
