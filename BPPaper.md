# Introduction

Pollution in the sea is often patchy, short-lived, and chemically complex. Even when surface waters look clear, contaminants may be subsurface, rapidly diluted below analytical detection limits, or missed by physical sensors, whether in situ (e.g., gliders) or remote (e.g., satellites) (Berenshtein et al., 2020). Chemical assays can provide precise measurements, but they frequently miss transient events or contaminants outside targeted detection ranges. Moreover, targeted chemical assays typically require prior knowledge of which pollutant to measure among thousands of possible compounds. These constraints motivate biological indicators that register organismal responses even when contaminants are intermittent, diluted, or outside chemical and physical detectability (Ferreira et al., 2023; Yarsan & Yipe, 2013).

Microbial communities are promising indicators because they turn over rapidly, interact directly with dissolved and particulate matter, and shift in composition on timescales relevant to exposure (Anthony et al., 2023; Fossi et al., 2000). High-throughput 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing—especially at amplicon sequence variant (ASV) resolution—captures these shifts in stable, single-nucleotide units that are reusable across time and datasets, enabling sensitive detection of compositional change (Jurburg, 2025). Pollution events—especially hydrocarbon spills and repeated or chronic contamination—can leave detectable community and functional signatures that outlast direct chemical or physical detection (Bik et al., 2012; George et al., 2025; Potts et al., 2022). Although microbial monitoring still often relies on ship-based sampling, autonomous samplers that preserve nucleic acids in situ are expanding temporal coverage and better matching the intermittency of pollution events (Knapik et al., 2020).

Interpreting any single field sample, however, remains challenging because ASV occurrence and relative abundance also vary with seasonality, shifting water masses, and intrinsic ecological variability. Long-term baselines address this problem by defining statistical normalcy—the expected distribution of community states under typical conditions—so that deviations can be evaluated against a historical range rather than against a single comparator (Banchi et al., 2022; Ji et al., 2024). In this sense, a time-series baseline effectively turns the microbial community into a long-term sensor, enabling baseline-referenced detection of statistically unusual ASV abundance patterns rather than relying on single–time-point differences.

In the Eastern Mediterranean Sea (EMS), a strong winter storm in February 2021 drove weathered oil ashore along ~180 km of Israel’s southeastern Levantine coast. Deposition was highly patchy, and rough seas hindered satellite detection and delayed glider missions (Herut, 2021). The 2021 tar pollution event prompted rapid-response efforts that included coastal campaigns across impacted areas and survey cruises during and after the event. Size-fractionated microbial DNA was sampled across depths along predicted pollution trajectories and from impacted coastal sites.

In oligotrophic seas, microbial responses are often structured by habitat, motivating separate analyses of free-living (FL; 0.22–11 µm) and particle-associated (PA; >11 µm) fractions (Lauro et al., 2009). This fractionation is particularly relevant for petroleum exposure because oil droplets, oil–particle aggregates, and weathered tar residues behave physically as particles and create discrete, colonizable surfaces. Under storm-forced conditions, however, such signals are unlikely to be vertically homogeneous. Instead, particle redistribution, transient aggregation, and trapping near density interfaces can localize biologically relevant exposure into depth-restricted hotspots, such that the strongest microbial response may appear in a particular fraction and depth horizon rather than as a smooth whole-water-column transition (Herut et al., 2024; Tedetti et al., 2013).

A central challenge in candidate microbial indicator discovery is specificity. Oil introduces a complex mixture of hydrocarbons that can function as both substrates and stressors (Head et al., 2006), yet many taxa that increase after oil exposure also bloom during natural pulses of dissolved organic matter (DOM), especially during the release of phytoplankton exudates in seasonal blooms. Opportunistic copiotrophs such as Alteromonadales, Flavobacteriales, and Rhodobacterales can respond strongly to both petroleum-derived and biogenic inputs (Head et al., 2006). As a result, oil-driven responses can be obscured unless non-oil DOM controls are included and field interpretation is anchored to robust baselines that capture background variability (Bagi et al., 2022; Knapik et al., 2020; Roth Rosenberg et al., 2021; Shai et al., 2021).

Here, we ask two linked questions: (1) Can ASV-level responses distinguish oil-derived carbon from generic organic enrichment under controlled conditions? (2) If so, are those responses detectable in spill-context field samples from the 2021 EMS tar event? We used a 48-h microcosm seeded with natural EMS seawater and compared four treatments: weathered tar, pyrene, phytoplankton exudates, and an unamended control. We analyzed PA and FL fractions separately and paired community changes with bacterial production (BP), primary production (PP), and N₂ fixation to probe underlying trophic processes. Candidate microbial indicators were nominated using conservative, multi-method differential-abundance consensus in the microcosms, then contextualized against the Southeastern Mediterranean Monthly Series (SoMMoS; 2018–2019) baseline by testing for exceedance of 99th-quantile, fraction-specific expected ranges. Because the field samples lack paired hydrocarbon chemistry, we treat these signals as candidate indicators rather than chemically confirmed indicators. We further examine these candidate indicators in an independent regional dataset that includes sampling during the 2021 event time frame and interpret them in the context of depth-resolved physical–biogeochemical structure during storm forcing.

# Methodology

## Study design overview

We combined (i) archived, size-fractionated field samples collected during and after the February 2021 EMS tar pollution event with (ii) a controlled 48-h microcosm designed to separate oil-derived amendment effects from generic DOM enrichment. Candidate ASV indicators were nominated from the experiment and then screened in pollution-context field samples by testing for fraction-specific exceedance relative to a long-term regional baseline (SoMMoS). We further compared the vertical structure of candidate ASVs across Cruise 2 and an independent (IOLR) dataset sampled during the same event window to assess whether field anomalies were distributed coherently through the water column or concentrated in depth-localized, fraction-specific horizons. An overview is provided in Fig. 1; procedural details are provided in Supplementary Methods.

## Field sampling and baseline context

Pollution-context samples were collected during the 2021 rapid-response effort and in a 2022 follow-up, spanning offshore-to-nearshore locations and including field blanks. Communities were collected by size-fractionated filtration to resolve particle-associated (PA; >11 µm) and free-living (FL; 0.22–11 µm) fractions. To contextualize event samples, we used the Southeastern Mediterranean Monthly Series (SoMMoS) time series as a baseline, which includes depth-resolved sampling at two EMS stations across ~180 samples. Baseline distributions were used to define candidate-specific expected ranges for anomaly screening (see below).

In addition, we analyzed an independent, non-size-fractionated 16S rRNA dataset provided by the **IOLR**, collected in parallel during the 2021 EMS pollution event. This independent snapshot was anchored to the **Haifa Transect Time Series** baseline to evaluate the distribution of our candidate microbial indicators across depths.
## Microcosm experiment and treatments

Surface seawater was collected offshore (Levantine Basin) and incubated for ~48 h under near in situ temperature and a diel light regime. Four treatments were established in biological replicate bottles: weathered tar, pyrene (DMSO carrier), phytoplankton exudates (0.22 µm filtered), and an unamended control. A preliminary tar dose pilot and pyrene axenic culture assays were performed to inform amendment concentrations (Supplementary Methods). Rate measurements (flow cytometry for cell abundance, bacterial production by leucine incorporation, primary production by ^14C assimilation, and N₂ fixation by ^15N₂ uptake) were conducted alongside community sampling.

## DNA sequencing and processing

At T0 and at the incubation endpoint, community DNA was collected by sequential filtration into PA and FL fractions; negative controls (filtration blanks and extraction blanks) were processed alongside samples. 16S rRNA amplicons (V4–V5 region) were sequenced with negative and positive controls. Reads were processed using standard denoising to generate ASVs, taxonomically annotated against SILVA, and analyzed within a phylogenetic/multivariate framework (Supplementary Methods).

## Contamination control and community analyses

We evaluated contamination using available field negative and extraction negative controls and applied a conservative decontamination using decontam (with most p-threshold = 0.5000), emphasizing prevalence-based detection within relevant subsets. Community composition was analyzed with Bray–Curtis dissimilarities and NMDS ordination, and group differences were tested using PERMANOVA with appropriate multiple-testing control.

## Differential abundance and candidate indicator screening

To improve robustness to method-specific artifacts, differential abundance (DA) was assessed using multiple frameworks and candidates were defined conservatively. Within each fraction, candidates were nominated from the microcosm as ASVs that were treatment-specific and supported by directionally consistent results in ≥2 DA methods, excluding signatures attributable to bottle effects (e.g., seawater T0 vs control). For field screening, each candidate was evaluated against SoMMoS baseline distributions by defining fraction-specific high-quantile thresholds (Q95/Q99) of non-zero relative abundances. Pollution-context samples were flagged when candidate ASVs exceeded baseline thresholds in the corresponding fraction; statistical support relative to baseline was assessed using prevalence- and abundance-based nonparametric tests with false-discovery-rate correction. Because per-sample hydrocarbon chemistry was not available, field signals are interpreted as candidate indicators rather than chemically confirmed indicators.

# Results

## Dose calibration in preliminary assays

A pilot tar incubation (0.5–4 g L⁻¹, added as tar balls) produced dose-dependent increases in cell abundance and bacterial production (BP), with the strongest response at 4 g L⁻¹ and the weakest at 0.5 g L⁻¹ (Supplementary Fig. S1). By ~68 h, BP at 0.5 g L⁻¹ approached control levels, whereas higher doses peaked earlier and remained elevated. Based on these trends, we selected 0.5 g L⁻¹ for the main microcosm as the lowest dose that yielded a measurable response in the pilot. In axenic cyanobacteria (*Prochlorococcus* MED4, MIT9301, MIT9313; *Synechococcus* WH8102), pyrene exposure (0–1000 µg L⁻¹) reduced growth in a concentration-dependent manner, while DMSO carrier controls had negligible effects (Supplementary Fig. S3). To minimize overt toxicity in mixed communities, we used 170 ng L⁻¹ pyrene in the 48-h incubation.

## 48-h incubation responses in cell counts and rates

We conducted a 48-h incubation with four treatments—weathered tar (0.5 g L⁻¹), pyrene (170 ng L⁻¹; DMSO carrier), *Prochlorococcus* MED4 exudates (3.1% v/v), and an unamended control—using offshore EMS surface seawater (32.96°N, 34.46°E). Exudates produced the strongest system-level response: heterotrophic and phytoplankton cell abundances increased by 24 h and peaked at 48 h, and BP increased by approximately one order of magnitude relative to the control at 24 h before partially relaxing by 48 h (Fig. X). Tar and pyrene produced smaller changes in cell abundances; however, BP increased at 24 h in both treatments and remained elevated at 48 h relative to the control (Fig. X).

Primary production (PP) increased transiently under exudates at 24 h and returned toward control values by 48 h, whereas PP was lower under tar and pyrene across 24–48 h (Fig. X). N₂ fixation rates were low overall but were higher in amended treatments than in the control and ranked exudates > tar > pyrene > control (Supplementary Fig. S4).

## Contamination filtering reduced negative-control signal while preserving sample structure

Negative controls contained substantial reads prior to filtering (Supplementary Fig. S2A). We applied conservative contaminant removal using prevalence- and/or frequency-based criteria within relevant subsets, removing ASVs flagged as contaminants. This workflow eliminated ~9.1% of total reads, reduced negative-control libraries to near zero, and preserved expected composition in positive controls (Supplementary Fig. S2B). Ordination patterns in the incubation subset were qualitatively similar before and after filtering, with slightly tighter clustering by treatment after filtering.

## Community composition shifts were dominated by treatment and size fraction, with exudates most distinct

After decontamination, Bray–Curtis NMDS ordinations showed good fit (Fig. Y). In analyses combining PA and FL fractions, both fraction and treatment explained community variation (PERMANOVA: fraction R²≈0.18, p=0.001; treatment R²≈0.31, p=0.001). Pairwise comparisons indicated that exudate communities differed from the control and from pyrene after multiple-testing correction, whereas tar and pyrene did not consistently separate from the control. When fractions were analyzed separately, treatment effects remained evident in both PA and FL, with greater dispersion in FL (Fig. Y).

## Differential-abundance consensus identified broad exudate responses and a focused tar response in PA

Differential abundance (DA) calls varied substantially across frameworks, but applying a multi-method consensus reduced method-specific signals to a compact, reproducible responder set (Fig. Z). Exudates produced the broadest response: in PA, 11 ASVs increased and 25 decreased by consensus; in FL, 21 increased and 45 decreased. Enriched taxa included multiple copiotrophic groups (e.g., Pseudomonadales, Flavobacteriales, Rhodobacterales). Representative high-effect examples included *Thalassotalea* (Colwelliaceae) ASV866 in PA (log₂FC=8.997; padj=5.6×10⁻⁷) and *Marinomonas* ASV42 in FL (log₂FC=8.195; p=2.5×10⁻¹⁶). The archaeon Ca. *Nitrosopelagicus* was consistently depleted under the exudate amendment.

Tar elicited a focused response confined to the PA fraction: two Alteromonadaceae ASVs were consistently enriched, *Glaciecola* ASV67 (log₂FC=5.53; padj=2.2×10⁻³) and an unclassified Alteromonadaceae ASV384 (log₂FC=5.19; padj=6.0×10⁻³) (Fig. Z). No ASV met the multi-method consensus for pyrene. In T0 vs control contrasts, bottle-effect shifts were detected and excluded from candidate nomination.

## Candidate microbial indicators: incubation nomination, baseline anchoring, and field exceedance

Candidate microbial indicators were nominated from incubation DA results as ASVs that were (i) enriched with ≥2-method support, (ii) treatment-specific, and (iii) not attributable to bottle effects. This yielded 26 nominated ASVs: 24 in FL (exudate-nominated) and 2 in PA (tar-nominated: ASV67 and ASV384). No pyrene-nominated candidates were identified.

We evaluated these candidates against SoMMoS baseline distributions using fraction-specific Q95/Q99 thresholds derived from non-zero relative abundances. Across pollution-context samples, 10 of 26 candidates exceeded their fraction-specific baseline threshold at least once. Eight candidates exceeded baseline in the same fraction in which they were nominated, whereas the two tar-nominated PA candidates exceeded baseline only in the opposite fraction (FL). Among baseline-exceeding candidates, a subset showed FDR-significant support by Fisher and/or Wilcoxon tests within fraction, and an additional subset was supported by an independent field-vs-baseline DA contrast (Fig. W). Together, these results summarize the pipeline from incubation nomination → baseline exceedance → statistical support in spill-context samples. To understand how those anomalies were structured in the water column, we next examined their depth distribution across Cruise 2 and an independent regional dataset sampled during the same event window.

## Cruise 2 depth structure reveals a particle-associated hotspot at ~240 m

Herut et al. (2024) provide an important physical–biogeochemical framework for interpreting Cruise 2 (the “tar cruise”) beyond taxonomy alone. In a rapid-response glider mission launched on 23 February 2021, but delayed by severe storm conditions, they identified short-lived, depth-resolved anomalies in PAH-like fluorophores (including naphthalene-like signals), tryptophan-like fluorophores, and particulate backscatter (bbp700). Two aspects of that record are especially relevant here. First, the strongest fluorophore anomalies were concentrated in the upper ~200 m, although they extended through much of the water column during the first 1–2 days. Second, bbp700 showed a clear vertical structure, with a build-up toward ~200 m followed by a sharper decline below, a pattern more consistent with particle or aggregate dynamics than with a vertically uniform “water-mass contamination” signal. Herut et al. further speculate that an intermediate-density layer near the LIW salinity maximum may act as a trapping horizon, where organic- or tar-associated particles become approximately neutrally buoyant and accumulate before further degradation or aggregation (Herut et al., 2024; Tedetti et al., 2013).

This physical picture matches the main biological pattern observed in Cruise 2: the strongest signal is fraction-specific and depth-localized, consistent with episodic particle capture rather than a vertically coherent shift in the free-living community. The clearest compositional change occurs in the particle-associated 11 µm fraction at ~240 m, where evenness declines and a few copiotrophic ASVs dominate, while the paired Sterivex fraction remains comparatively even and closer to baseline. Consistent with this, several candidate ASVs in Cruise 2 show local maxima in the particle-associated fraction at 240 m. One notable example is ASV67 (_Glaciecola_), which is nearly absent across other depths and fractions but appears at 240 m in the PA fraction, despite not being flagged as enriched relative to the SoMMoS baseline. A more mixed pattern is seen for other candidate ASVs. ASV180 (_Pseudoalteromonas_) peaks in both fractions at 120 m, with a secondary peak at 240 m, yet it likewise does not exceed the SoMMoS baseline strongly enough to be classified as enriched. ASV236 (_Aurantivirga_) and ASV448 (NS3a marine group) both show depth-structured peaks across the free-living and particle-associated fractions, but with different vertical offsets: ASV236 reaches its main peak in the free-living fraction at 120 m, drops to nearly zero there at 240 m while peaking instead in the particle-associated fraction, and then rebounds below 280 m; ASV448 shows a complementary pattern, peaking in the particle-associated fraction at 120 m and then in the free-living fraction at 240 m. Both ASV236 and ASV448 are also present in the most superficial waters at moderate relative abundances. Taken together, these profiles indicate that the Cruise 2 response was not a simple, uniform enrichment of particular taxa throughout the water column, but rather a depth-specific and fraction-dependent redistribution.

## Independent MAX (IOLR) samples show shallow, patchy candidate-ASV exceedances

The MAX (IOLR) tar-cruise dataset provides a second, independent, depth-resolved window into the same event period. In the MAX tar-cruise samples (HZ_2021_02_Tar), biomarker exceedances are confined to a subset of shallow depths and, critically, are not restricted to the tar-linked candidate ASV set. Instead, they include candidate lineages more plausibly interpreted as responders to labile DOM and particle-associated niches.

Two aspects of the MAX exceedance pattern are particularly informative. First, the NS3a marine group candidate ASV448 shows surface-intensified exceedances at 1 m and 3 m, consistent with an upper-ocean envelope of biologically legible disturbance during the response window and with the idea that near-surface waters can carry strong DOM and particle signals even when deeper layers exhibit different dynamics. Second, the putatively tar-linked _Glaciecola_ ASV67 exceeds at 15 m and 30 m, but it does not simply track the NS3a signal: ASV67 exceeds alone at 15 m, while co-occurring with ASV448 only at 30 m. This non-obligate co-occurrence indicates that the observed field signal is mixed rather than expressed as a single, uniform candidate-ASV assemblage across depths.

# Discussion

This study tested whether ASV-level 16S rRNA responses can discriminate oil-derived amendments (weathered tar, pyrene) from generic organic enrichment (phytoplankton exudates) under controlled conditions, and whether laboratory-nominated candidates can be detected as baseline-referenced anomalies in spill-context field samples from the 2021 EMS tar event. Two conclusions emerge. First, labile DOM drove the dominant compositional response, and many high-amplitude shifts occurred in taxa that are not oil-specific. Second, tar elicited a narrow, fraction-linked signal: two Alteromonadaceae ASVs, including _Glaciecola_ ASV67, were enriched exclusively in tar-amended particle-associated (PA) samples under a conservative multi-method consensus rule. Together, these findings indicate that highly specific ASV-level responses can occur, but were rare under our short-duration, low-dose conditions and conservative nomination criteria and difficult to translate unambiguously to field settings lacking paired chemistry.

## DOM responses limit oil specificity at ASV resolution

Phytoplankton exudates produced the strongest system-level response, consistent with rapid copiotrophic blooms following pulses of labile organic matter (Bachi et al., 2023; Buchan et al., 2014; Kieft et al., 2021; Teeling et al., 2012). This pattern was evident in increased BP and rising cell abundances over 24–48 h, alongside broad compositional shifts. Exudates enriched classic copiotrophic lineages (e.g., Pseudomonadales, Flavobacteriales, Rhodobacterales and related Gammaproteobacteria) while oligotroph-associated groups (e.g., SAR11 and ammonia-oxidizing archaea such as Ca. _Nitrosopelagicus_) declined, consistent with bloom-associated replacement of oligotrophic background taxa by fast-growing heterotrophs when labile DOM becomes abundant (Buchan et al., 2014; Kieft et al., 2021; S. Liu et al., 2020; Teeling et al., 2012).

A key implication is that many lineages frequently reported as “early oil responders” are also canonical responders to non-oil DOM pulses, especially in oligotrophic waters where opportunists respond rapidly to labile inputs regardless of source (Head et al., 2006; Joye et al., 2016; Buchan et al., 2014). In our incubation, this overlap was reflected in modest separation of tar treatments from controls in ordination space and in shared directions of change for some copiotrophic groups, consistent with observations that opportunistic taxa bloom under petroleum-derived or biogenic organic enrichment (Joye et al., 2016; Knapik et al., 2020). Operationally, this constrains the usefulness of single-genus (or single-ASV) indicators for oil exposure in oligotrophic waters during periods when natural DOM pulses are common. Specificity is more likely to come from (i) multi-taxon panels evaluated jointly, (ii) longer time windows that capture successional turnover toward specialists, and/or (iii) functional markers that directly reflect hydrocarbon metabolism (Bagi et al., 2022; Knapik et al., 2020).

## Why did tar and pyrene yield limited ASV-level signals?

Several non-exclusive constraints likely contributed to the weak oil-specific signal. First, dose and exposure duration may have limited the emergence of detectable specialist blooms. Tar and pyrene concentrations were selected to avoid overt toxicity, but may have been below thresholds required to produce rapid, high-amplitude growth of hydrocarbon specialists within 48 h; alternatively, they may have imposed stress that dampened community-level growth responses (Cerezo & Agusti, 2015; Echeveste et al., 2010; Haritash & Kaushik, 2009; Headrick et al., 2023). More broadly, experimental and mesocosm work shows microbial community outcomes can be strongly dose-dependent (Aljandal et al., 2022). Second, hydrocarbon-degrader succession often includes multi-day lags shaped by nutrient context, substrate accessibility, and competition; a 48-h window may capture early generalist dynamics while missing later peaks of obligate hydrocarbonoclastic bacteria (e.g., _Alcanivorax_ for alkanes and _Cycloclasticus_ for PAHs) (Gutierrez et al., 2013; Head et al., 2006; Joye et al., 2016; Knapik et al., 2020).

Pyrene represents a particularly strict test because it is a single compound added at low concentration. Under these conditions, pyrene likely contributed negligible growth-supporting carbon while still exerting stress on phototrophs; consistent with this, pyrene produced no multi-method consensus enrichments. In short incubations, low-dose PAH additions may therefore manifest primarily as stress (depletions and suppressed photosynthetic performance) rather than as a detectable bloom of PAH specialists (Haritash & Kaushik, 2009; Head et al., 2006; Joye et al., 2016).

We also note a broader uncertainty in how representative our tar exposure was of the field. Few controlled studies amend seawater with weathered tar particles at volumetric doses comparable to ours; most micro-/mesocosm studies use crude oil/dispersed oil rather than “tar balls.” Field work on weathered tar often focuses on beach accumulations rather than water-column concentrations, and the ambient free-water tar burden during spill conditions remains poorly constrained (Owens et al., 2002; Peters & Siuda, 2014; Warnock et al., 2015). Thus, the in situ exposure of seawater microbes to tar particles during the 2021 event may have been substantially lower, or in a different particulate form/dispersion state, than in our treatment.

## Functional responses provide context but are not, by themselves, a diagnostic

Although compositional shifts under tar and pyrene were subtle, both amendments coincided with suppressed PP across 24–48 h relative to controls, while BP increased relative to controls. This BP↑/PP↓ pattern is consistent with petroleum-derived inputs acting as both substrate for subsets of heterotrophs and stressor for phototroph physiology, but in tar-amended bottles it could also reflect physical and optical effects of tar particles (e.g., shading or adsorption artifacts) that we did not explicitly control for. Similar bidirectional outcomes—stimulation of some heterotrophs alongside inhibition of primary producers—have been reported and depend on dose, oil chemistry, and photochemical history (Head et al., 2006; Headrick et al., 2023; Joye et al., 2016). In our dataset, exudates represent labile carbon enrichment associated with strong heterotrophic and phototrophic increases, whereas tar and pyrene coincided with reduced autotrophic performance.

At the same time, this functional pattern should be treated as contextual rather than diagnostic. Petroleum effects are not directionally uniform: depending on dose and chemistry, aged oils can inhibit heterotrophic production as well (Headrick et al., 2023). Thus, coupled functional measurements (BP, PP, N₂ fixation) can increase interpretability, but require validation across longer durations, broader dose ranges, and chemically characterized amendments before being treated as discriminators (Head et al., 2006; Joye et al., 2016). More generally, functional outcomes may help distinguish “modes” of perturbation but are not, alone, proof of petroleum exposure.

## Fractionation supports particle-linked tar responses in the microcosm

A defining feature of the tar signal was fraction specificity. The two tar-associated ASVs (_Glaciecola_ ASV67 and an unclassified Alteromonadaceae ASV384) were enriched only in tar-amended PA samples. This is physically plausible given that weathered tar provides discrete colonizable surfaces and particle-associated microenvironments; correspondingly, the lack of a free-living enrichment suggests that dissolved-phase hydrocarbons were insufficient to drive a planktonic bloom within 48 h (Head et al., 2006; Lauro et al., 2009).

However, mechanistic interpretation remains limited by the physical form of the amendment. Tar was added as millimeter-scale tar balls, whereas the PA fraction is operationally defined by retention on an 11 µm filter. The observed enrichment could reflect fragmentation into smaller particles or colloids, detachment of colonizers from tar surfaces, and/or formation of tar–particle aggregates. Without particle size distribution measurements during incubation, the most conservative interpretation is that the tar-associated signal is consistent with particle-linked ecology rather than direct evidence of tar-ball colonization per se. Because we did not include an inert particle control matched to tarball size/optics, we cannot fully separate hydrocarbon-specific effects from generic particle-associated enrichment.

The ecology of _Glaciecola_ also complicates inference: the genus has been reported to respond to both petroleum inputs and phytoplankton-derived DOM (Cerezo & Agusti, 2015; Krolicka et al., 2017; Knapik et al., 2020). Thus, the selective enrichment of ASV67 only under tar in our microcosm nominates it as a putatively tar-responsive variant within a genus that can participate in both DOM and oil-related dynamics.

The MAX (IOLR) tar-cruise dataset adds a second, independent depth-resolved window into the same event period and strengthens the “particle/DOM pulse + patchiness” interpretation. In the MAX tar-cruise samples (HZ_2021_02_Tar), biomarker exceedances are confined to a subset of shallow depths and, critically, are not restricted to the tar-linked candidate ASV set. Instead, they include candidate lineages more plausibly interpreted as responders to labile DOM and particle-associated niches. Two aspects of the MAX exceedance pattern are particularly informative. First, the NS3a marine group candidate ASV448 shows surface-intensified exceedances at 1 m and 3 m, consistent with an upper-ocean envelope of biologically legible disturbance during the response window and with the idea that near-surface waters can carry strong DOM and particle signals even when deeper layers exhibit different dynamics. Second, the putatively tar-linked _Glaciecola_ ASV67 exceeds at 15 m and 30 m, but it does not simply track the NS3a signal: ASV67 exceeds alone at 15 m, while co-occurring with ASV448 only at 30 m. This non-obligate co-occurrence supports a mixed-forcing interpretation rather than a single, uniform “tar community state.” More specifically, ASV448 appears to represent a broader, more frequently expressed surface response consistent with labile DOM and particle-associated niches, whereas ASV67 behaves as a sparser, more depth-localized signal that sometimes, but not always, rides on top of that broader response when tar-derived material and organic-matter or particle enrichment coincide.

Taken together, the Cruise 2 and MAX observations converge on the same ecological mechanism suggested by the operational oceanography context: the most prominent microbial excursions during the tar-event window are best understood as episodic, particle-bound hotspots expressed at discrete depths, with mixed panel membership reflecting the reality that storm-forced systems can simultaneously redistribute tar residues and intensify particle-associated organic-enrichment niches. Within this framework, detecting a tar-linked ASV at a subset of depths remains meaningful, but the stronger inference comes from the structure of the signal itself—shallow, patchy, and fraction- or particle-consistent—rather than from expecting a smooth, depth-consistent “tar-like” fingerprint across size fractions. This “particle hotspot” framing is exactly what the glider context predicts during and after storm forcing: patchy aggregates and particle plumes can generate strong microbial excursions at discrete depths even when the surrounding bulk water does not show an equivalently sharp biological transition.

## Lab-to-field translation is constrained by missing chemistry and baseline mismatch

Field validation is limited primarily by the absence of per-sample hydrocarbon chemistry in spill-context samples. Consequently, field screening should be interpreted as baseline-referenced anomaly detection rather than confirmation of pollutant causality (Joye et al., 2016). Using SoMMoS as a baseline provides a principled way to define expected ASV abundance ranges and to flag statistically unusual exceedances, but mismatch remains: SoMMoS stations differ from nearshore impacted habitats, and baseline years (2018–2019) do not fully capture 2021 conditions (Reich et al., 2022). These differences can inflate or deflate exceedance probabilities for taxa with strong coastal or seasonal structure.

A notable lab–field discrepancy was that the two tar-nominated PA candidates exceeded baseline primarily in the FL fraction in field samples. One plausible explanation is that hydrocarbon physical state and dispersion differ strongly between bottle incubations and the ocean: weathering, emulsification, shear, and biological processing can generate micro-/colloidal droplets and oil–particle aggregates that pass 11 µm yet are retained on 0.22 µm filters, shifting the operational “interaction space” toward FL size classes as oil ages and disperses (Hickl et al., 2023; White et al., 2020). Because particle distributions were not measured in either setting, this remains a hypothesis rather than a resolved mechanism, and it reinforces the need for paired physical/chemical context when interpreting fraction-localized signatures. The new field observations suggest, however, that at least some apparent lab–field mismatch may reflect real ecological structure rather than simple analytical inconsistency: storm-forced patchiness, transient trapping horizons, and rapid exchange between particle-associated and operationally free-living material can redistribute event-related microbial signals across fractions and depths. At the same time, fraction inversion could also arise from methodological differences in size fractionation (filter clogging, carryover between sequential filters), baseline habitat mismatch (nearshore vs offshore), or storm-driven particle/DOM inputs unrelated to petroleum; without paired chemistry and particle measurements, the mechanism remains unresolved.

## Stringent contamination control improves confidence but reduces sensitivity

Low-biomass contamination and bleed-through are persistent risks in amplicon datasets, particularly when multiple sample origins are pooled and negatives contain substantial reads (Davis et al., 2018; Eisenhofer et al., 2019). Our conservative decontamination and filtering strategy reduced negative-control signal and prioritized high-confidence candidate nomination, but it also reduces sensitivity and statistical power. Slow-growing or low-abundance responders—especially obligate hydrocarbonoclasts—could remain undetected within 48 h or be removed by conservative feature filtering. Accordingly, the absence of canonical hydrocarbon degraders in this dataset should be interpreted as “not detected under these conditions,” not as evidence of absence (Head et al., 2006; Joye et al., 2016).

## Implications for microbial indicators of oil exposure in oligotrophic seas

Overall, these results suggest that ASV-level 16S signals alone are unlikely to yield a universal “yes/no” oil indicator under realistic ecological variability in oligotrophic waters. A more defensible approach is probabilistic: nominate candidates under controlled contrasts that explicitly include non-oil DOM enrichment; evaluate candidates as anomalies against long-term baselines; and increase specificity by integrating functional measurements and targeted functional markers (e.g., alkane monooxygenases such as _alkB_ and PAH-dioxygenases), alongside approaches that improve quantitative interpretability (e.g., spike-ins, qPCR, or metagenomic read-based abundance estimates) (Bagi et al., 2022; Gloor et al., 2017; Tourlousse et al., 2016). This type of multi-method triangulation is valuable because DA outcomes can depend strongly on analysis choices and data properties (Aitchison, 1982; Gloor et al., 2017; Weiss et al., 2017). Within this framework, tar-associated Alteromonadaceae ASVs represent plausible candidates for further validation, whereas the broader exudate-linked panel captures carbon-enrichment disturbance that is not uniquely petroleum-derived.

The present field results refine that conclusion further: in oligotrophic EMS waters, meaningful event signals may be expressed less as broad, vertically coherent taxonomic fingerprints than as depth-localized, fraction-specific particle hotspots. Natural DOM pulses can reproduce much of the early microbial response commonly attributed to oil (Head et al., 2006; Joye et al., 2016), but storm-forced systems can also redistribute tar residues and organic-rich particles in ways that generate sharp biological excursions at discrete depths. Practical microbial monitoring will therefore require baseline-anchored anomaly detection coupled to functional, chemical, and physical context, including explicit attention to size fraction and vertical structure, rather than reliance on single taxonomic indicators alone (Joye et al., 2016; Reich et al., 2022).
