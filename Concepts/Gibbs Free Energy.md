## Thermodynamics: Spontaneity, Enthalpy, and Entropy

1. Key Definition

• Energetically Favorable: Refers to a spontaneous process where Gibbs Free Energy decreases ($\Delta G < 0$).  

• Exothermic ($\Delta H < 0$): Heat is released by the system to the surroundings.
• Endothermic ($\Delta H > 0$): Heat is absorbed by the system from the surroundings.
• Entropically Favorable ($\Delta S_{sys} > 0$): The entropy (disorder) of the system increases.
• Entropically Unfavorable ($\Delta S_{sys} < 0$): The entropy of the system decreases (becomes more ordered).

  

2. The Thermodynamic Laws

  

• First Law: Energy is conserved ($\Delta U = Q - W$).

  

• Second Law: The entropy of the universe is always increasing ($\Delta S_{univ} > 0$) for any spontaneous process. Even if a system becomes ordered, it must release enough heat to the surroundings to increase their entropy even more.

  

3. The Gibbs Free Energy Equation

  

 The T factor determines the weight of the entropy term:

  

• At High Temperatures: The $T\Delta S$ term dominates. Entropy-driven reactions become more favorable.

  

• At Low Temperatures: The $\Delta H$ term dominates. Enthalpy (heat) determines spontaneity.

  

4. Examples

  

A. Exothermic & Entropically Favorable (Always Spontaneous)

  

• Example: Combustion of Wood.

  

• Why: It releases heat ($\Delta H < 0$) and turns a solid into many gas molecules ($\Delta S > 0$). It happens easily at any temperature once started.

  

B. Endothermic but Entropically Favorable (Spontaneous at High T)

  

• Example: Ice Melting.

  

• Why: It takes heat to melt ice ($\Delta H > 0$), but liquid water is more disordered than ice ($\Delta S > 0$). At low temperatures ($<0^\circ C$), it's not spontaneous; at high temperatures ($>0^\circ C$), the $T\Delta S$ term wins and it melts.

  

C. Exothermic but Entropically Unfavorable (Spontaneous at Low T)

  

• Example: Water Freezing.

  

• Why: Freezing releases heat ($\Delta H < 0$) but creates order ($\Delta S < 0$). This only happens at low temperatures where the "order-making" penalty ($T\Delta S$) is small enough for the heat release to compensate.
