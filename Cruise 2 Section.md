### **Linking operational oceanography signals to microbial “hotspots” in Cruise 2 and the IOLR tar-cruise: why the strongest biological signal can be particle-bound, depth-structured, and not uniquely “tar-like”**


Herut et al. (2024) provide an important physical–biogeochemical framework for interpreting Cruise 2 (the “tar cruise”) beyond taxonomy alone. In a rapid-response glider mission launched on 23 February 2021, but delayed by severe storm conditions, they identified short-lived, depth-resolved anomalies in PAH-like fluorophores (including naphthalene-like signals), tryptophan-like fluorophores, and particulate backscatter (bbp700). Two aspects of that record are especially relevant here. First, the strongest fluorophore anomalies were concentrated in the upper ~200 m, although they extended through much of the water column during the first 1–2 days. Second, bbp700 showed a clear vertical structure, with a build-up toward ~200 m followed by a sharper decline below, a pattern more consistent with particle or aggregate dynamics than with a vertically uniform “water-mass contamination” signal. Herut et al. further speculate that an intermediate-density layer near the LIW salinity maximum may act as a trapping horizon, where organic or tar-associated particles become approximately neutrally buoyant and accumulate before subsequent degradation or aggregation. This physical picture matches the main biological pattern observed in Cruise 2: the strongest signal is fraction-specific and depth-localized, consistent with episodic particle capture rather than a vertically coherent shift in the free-living community. The clearest compositional change occurs in the particle-associated 11 µm fraction at ~240 m, where evenness declines and a few copiotrophic ASVs dominate, while the paired Sterivex fraction remains comparatively even and closer to baseline. Consistent with this, several candidate ASVs in Cruise 2 show local maxima in the particle-associated fraction at 240 m. One notable example is ASV67 (_Glaciecola_), which is nearly absent across other depths and fractions but appears at 240 m in the PA fraction, despite not being flagged as enriched relative to the SoMMoS baseline. A more mixed pattern is seen for other candidate ASVs. ASV180 (_Pseudoalteromonas_) peaks in both fractions at 120 m, with a secondary peak at 240 m, yet it likewise does not exceed the SoMMoS baseline strongly enough to be classified as enriched. ASV236 (_Aurantivirga_) and ASV448 (NS3a marine group) both show depth-structured peaks across the free-living and particle-associated fractions, but with different vertical offsets: ASV236 reaches its main peak in the free-living fraction at 120 m, drops to nearly zero in that fraction at 240 m while peaking instead in the particle-associated fraction, and then rebounds below 280 m; ASV448 shows a complementary pattern, peaking in the particle-associated fraction at 120 m and then in the free-living fraction at 240 m. Both ASV236 and ASV448 are also present in the most superficial waters at moderate relative abundances. Taken together, these profiles suggest that the Cruise 2 response was not a simple, uniform enrichment of particular taxa throughout the water column, but rather a depth-specific and fraction-dependent redistribution, consistent with transient particle aggregation, trapping, and exchange between free-living and particle-associated microbial niches. This “particle hotspot” framing is exactly what the glider context predicts we should expect during/after storm forcing: patchy aggregates and particle plumes can generate strong microbial excursions at discrete depths even when the surrounding bulk water does not show an equivalently sharp biological transition.

  

**MAX (IOLR) tar-cruise biomarkers: exceedances are shallow, patchy, and mix “tar-linked” and “exudate/particle-linked” signals**

The MAX (IOLR) tar-cruise dataset adds a second, independent depth-resolved window into the same event period and strengthens the “particle/DOM pulse + patchiness” interpretation. In the MAX tar-cruise samples (HZ_2021_02_Tar), biomarker exceedances are confined to a subset of shallow depths and, critically, are not restricted to the tar-linked candidate ASV set. Instead, they include candidate lineages that are more plausibly interpreted as responders to labile DOM and particle-associated niches.

Two aspects of the MAX exceedance pattern are particularly informative. First, the **NS3a marine group candidate ASV448** shows **surface-intensified exceedances at 1 m and 3 m**, consistent with an upper-ocean envelope of biologically legible disturbance during the response window and with the idea that near-surface waters can carry strong DOM/particle signals even when deeper layers show different dynamics. Second, the putatively tar-linked **Glaciecola ASV67** exceeds at **15 m and 30 m**, but it does not simply track the NS3a signal: **ASV67 exceeds alone at 15 m**, while **co-occurring with ASV448 only at 30 m**. This non-obligate co-occurrence supports a mixed-forcing interpretation rather than a single uniform “tar community state.” Specifically, ASV448 appears to represent a broader, more frequently expressed surface response consistent with labile DOM/particle niches, whereas ASV67 behaves as a sparser, more depth-localized signal that sometimes—but not always—rides on top of that broader response when tar-derived material and organic-matter/particle enrichment coincide.

Taken together, the Cruise 2 and MAX observations converge on the same ecological mechanism suggested by the operational oceanography context: the most prominent microbial excursions during the tar-event window are best framed as **episodic, particle-bound hotspots expressed at discrete depths**, with **mixed panel membership** reflecting the reality that storm-forced systems can simultaneously redistribute tar residues and intensify particle-associated organic enrichment niches. Under this framework, detecting a tar-linked ASV at a subset of depths remains meaningful, but the stronger inference comes from the _structure_ of the signal—shallow, patchy, and fraction/particle consistent—rather than from expecting a smooth, depth-consistent “tar-like” fingerprint across size fractions.
