We initiated a 16-day glider mission on 23 February 2021 as a response to the tar pollution event, starting in delay by 6 days due to a significant sea storm (>5 m significant wave height; measured at Hadera (Gloss #80) station). Nonetheless, anomalous values of PAHs-like and particulates (backscatter) were detected in its first day's mission, gradually returning to ambient background values at the 4th day (since 26 February 2021, Fig. 5). While the absolute concentrations detected by the glider sensors were not validated by chemical analysis of water samples, they show a clear depth-profile signal of naphthalene-like and tryptophan-like concentrations measured over the ~500 m isobath. The naphthalene-like and tryptophan-like vertical anomalies extended during the 23–24 February 2021 over the whole water column (till ~700 m), showing larger anomalies (by a factor of 2) in the upper 200 m as compared to the deeper waters (Fig. 5). A gradual return to background values (asserted from an open water measurement, i.e. cycle 175) is evident for both substances over a period of 4 days (23–26 February 2021). The spatial locations of these signals coincide with the schedule of the tar beaching event and model simulations of the slick propagation  (see Section 3.2). In the days afterward (26 February 2021 to 6 March 2021) a consistent vertical distribution was detected, representing the ambient/background concentrations of naphthalene-like (~0.4 and 0.3μg l 1 in the upper and deep water, respectively) and tryptophan-like  (~4 and ~3.3 μg l 1 in the upper and deep water, respectively). The tryptophan-like signals may represent both, tryptophan and/or fluorene-like fluorophores (Tedetti et al., 2013). The parallel depthdistribution of the naphthalene and tryptophan-like signals may signify here the contamination of fluorene by the oil spill and tar particles (Table 2). The particulate backscattering (bbp700) or particulate organic matter distribution show similar temporal anomalies as for the fluorophores signal, enhancement during the 23–24 February 2021 and return to background values afterward (Fig. 5), but with different depthprofile pattern. A gradual depth increase in particulate concentrations is observed till ~200 m, with a sharp decrease to background values in deeper water (Fig. 5). The mixed layer depth (MLD) at the Southeastern LB during the event dates was about 100 m (Fig. 5), shallower than the observed  fluorophores and suspended particulates distribution across the upper 200 m water mass. The 200 m depth backscattering peak coincide the Levantine Intermediate Water (LIW) maximal salinity peak on 19 February 2021 as observed by two ARGOS profiles located at the SE LB area (Fig. 5). Daily mean thermohaline profiles, averaged over the domain of 34–34.6◦ E and 32–33◦ N, from CMEMS physical reanalysis (Fig. S7) agree with the ARGOS profiles (Fig. 5). It is speculated that the maximal particle concentration at the 200 m depth layer is attributed to  the entrapment of organic particles at this density (1028.9 kg m 3) layer in which the density of seawater and the tar/organic particles balances, till further degradation or aggregation. Thus, the observed anomaly at the upper 200 m probably reflects the contamination by particle-derived PAHs and the enhancement of both, tar and bacterial particulates. The observed water column distribution and temporal changes in the intensity of the PAHs-like signals emphasize the advantage of an operational use of the glider for monitoring water quality and for validating suspected oil spills, especially in marine areas of high oil pollution risk like the LB. The glider enables detecting underwater oil signals, as opposed to satellite remote sensing (SRS) or surface water flight observations. Recently the operational use of underwater gliders to map oil spills in the ocean was suggested (Dhont et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2022).
