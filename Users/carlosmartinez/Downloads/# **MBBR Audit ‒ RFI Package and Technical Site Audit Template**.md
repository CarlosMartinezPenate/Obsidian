## **Introduction**
  
A successful audit of a Moving Bed Biofilm Reactor (MBBR) wastewater treatment plant hinges on understanding how the facility was designed to perform and how it is currently operating. Design flows and pollutant loads establish the **basis of design**, while actual flows and loads provide the benchmark for evaluating capacity and performance. In modern MBBR design, it is critical to evaluate **average and maximum flows** and to quantify **organic and nitrogen constituents** entering the first stage of treatment.  Waste loads must include BOD₅, TSS and nitrogen species (TKN, NH₄‑N, NO₃‑N); nitrification requires sufficient oxygen, and organic removal must occur before nitrification .  This Request for Information (RFI) and Technical Site Audit Template is structured in two phases to collect all necessary data and guide a systematic on‑site inspection.

### **2. Mechanical Inventory and Specifications**

  

Provide the following detailed information for all critical equipment associated with the MBBR system.  The aim is to verify capacity, assess remaining life, and identify possible bottlenecks.

  

#### **2.1 Blowers and Aeration System**

- **Blower make and model**, rated capacity (m³/h or cfm) and pressure.  Include blower curves and operating points.  Proper aeration is essential for biofilm health; inadequate oxygen delivery or uneven air distribution can reduce performance .
    
- **Number of blowers**, operating/standby configuration, installation date and service history.
    
- **Diffuser type** (fine bubble, coarse bubble, discs, tubes), material, and **age**.  Identify any history of fouling or clogging.  Diffusers should be accessible for maintenance; design should allow removal when replacement is necessary .
    
- **Aeration manifold and piping** details (diameter, material, presence of isolation valves or flow control devices).
    
- **Aeration control** (manual valves, auto‑DO control, VFDs).  Specify whether DO sensors are installed, their type (optical, membrane) and calibration frequency.
    

  

#### **2.2 Media Retention Sieves/Screens**

- **Location and number** of inlet and outlet screens/sieves; specify **slot opening widths** (mm) and materials.  Verify the integrity of sieves and any history of blinding or media loss .
    
- **Media retention system configuration** (e.g., cylindrical wedge‑wire sieves, flat screens).  Note any antifoam spray bars or spray cleaning systems.
    

  

#### **2.3 Biofilm Carriers**

- **Type and manufacturer** of carriers (e.g., K1, K3, chips, bio‑chip), including **geometry and protected surface area** (m²/m³).  High-quality carriers should provide ≥600 m²/m³ surface area and stable buoyancy .
    
- **Installed volume (m³)** and **fill fraction** (% of reactor volume).  Typical design fill fractions range 40–60 %; confirm design versus actual loaded volume.
    
- **Carrier age and condition**; note any observed discoloration, breakage, or loss.  Discoloration, breakage or uneven floating/sinking are red flags indicating poor media quality .
    

  

#### **2.4 Reactor Basins and Piping**

- **Number of stages** (aerobic, anoxic) and **volume of each basin**.
    
- **Materials of construction** (concrete, fiberglass, steel) and installation dates.
    
- **Internal mixing systems** (for anoxic basins) – type (submersible mixers, jet mixing), capacity and manufacturer.
    
- **Hydraulic retention time** (design vs. current based on actual flows).
    

  

#### **2.5 Instrumentation and Control**

- **DO, pH, ORP and temperature sensors** – make/model, number, location in each stage, calibration schedule.  Adequate monitoring is necessary for DO profiling and pH control .
    
- **Ammonia or nitrate online analysers**, if installed.
    
- **SCADA or data acquisition systems** – capabilities (trend recording, alarm functions).  Note any remote monitoring features.
    

  

### **3. Energy and Chemical Consumption Logs**

  

Provide at least 12 months of historical data (monthly or weekly) for:

- **Electrical consumption (kWh)** specifically attributable to aeration blowers, mixers and ancillary equipment.  Include peak demand values and any variation due to seasonal loadings.
    
- **Chemical usage**:
    
    - **Polymer dosage** – product type, dosage rate (kg/day), application point (e.g., coagulation, sludge conditioning).
        
    - **pH adjustment chemicals** (lime, caustic soda, acid), usage rates and frequency.  Controlling pH is critical for biofilm formation and stability .
        
    
- **Other additives** (carbon sources for denitrification, nutrients, defoamers) – type, dosage and procurement history.  Avoid silica‑based defoaming agents which may coat carriers and impede diffusion .
    

  

### **4. Operational and Maintenance Records**

- **Process control logs**: daily flow, DO, pH, temperature, ammonia/nitrate, sludge wasting volumes and return flow rates.
    
- **Preventive maintenance schedules and work orders** for blowers, diffusers, mixers, sieves and pumps.
    
- **Downtime events**, bypass events or any operation outside normal range (e.g., high‑load days, storm events) with descriptions.
    
- **Operator training records** and staffing levels.
    

  

### **5. Regulatory and Discharge Requirements**

- Copies of discharge permits or local standards specifying limits for BOD, COD, TSS, nitrogen species and other parameters.
    
- Any correspondence with regulators regarding non‑compliance events, capacity expansions or process modifications.

## **Phase 2 – Technical Site Audit Template**

  

This template is to be used on site in El Salvador after the RFI data have been reviewed.  It is organized into inspection, measurements and interviews.

  

### **1. Physical Inspection Checklist**

  

#### **1.1 Carriers / Biofilm Condition**

- **Color and Odor** – carriers should have a consistent biofilm color (light tan to brown).  **Discoloration or strong odors**, such as sulfur or septic smell, may indicate poor biofilm development or sloughing .
    
- **Floatation Behavior** – carriers should be neutrally buoyant.  **Uneven floating or sinking** causes dead zones and poor mixing .
    
- **Clumping/Aggregation** – carriers should move freely.  Clumping or carrier aggregation can lead to **uneven biofilm thickness**, impeding treatment efficiency .
    
- **Biofilm Thickness** – visually assess biofilm thickness; it should be thin and evenly distributed.  **Thick or uneven biofilms hinder substrate and oxygen transport** .  Use a marked ruler or callipers to measure typical biofilm thickness from randomly selected carriers.
    
- **Mechanical Integrity** – inspect for **breakage** or surface damage.  Frequent breakage is a red flag for low‑quality media .
    

  

#### **1.2 Reactor Basin and Screens**

- **Screens/Sieves** – inspect inlet/outlet screens for **blinding or clogging**.  Record slot widths and measure build‑up thickness.  Check spray bars or cleaning mechanisms for proper operation .
    
- **Aeration Grid** – visually inspect diffusers for fouling, damaged membranes or broken supports.  Check for even distribution of bubbles across the basin surface; uneven distribution may indicate clogged diffusers .
    
- **Mixers (anoxic zones)** – verify operation, vibration, and shaft/bearing condition.  Observe mixing patterns to ensure carrier circulation.
    
- **Structural Condition** – observe walls and floor for cracks, leaks, scaling or corrosion.  Check that internal baffles and weirs are intact and level.
    
- **Safety and Access** – ensure safe access to carriers, diffusers and screens for maintenance; presence of guardrails and fall protection.
    

  

### **2. Process Measurements**

  

To assess current performance and identify inefficiencies, conduct the following field tests during the audit:

- **Dissolved Oxygen (DO) Profiling** – measure DO at multiple depths and longitudinal locations within each MBBR stage.  Adequate oxygen supply and even distribution are critical .  Use portable DO meters and record temperature simultaneously.
    
- **pH and Temperature Monitoring** – measure pH and temperature at influent, each MBBR basin and effluent; confirm that pH remains within optimum range for biofilm formation (typically 6.5–8.0) .
    
- **SVI / Settling Tests** – collect samples of sloughed biomass from secondary clarifier underflow or MBBR effluent and perform **Sludge Volume Index (SVI)** or simple settling tests to determine settleability and floc characteristics.
    
- **Grab Sample Locations** – at minimum: pre‑screen influent, post‑screen influent (after primary treatment), MBBR effluent, downstream clarifier effluent and final discharge.  Additional intermediate samples (between MBBR stages) should be taken to evaluate nitrification and denitrification performance.
    
- **Ammonia and Nitrate Profiling** – conduct on-site measurements or send to laboratory for NH₄‑N, NO₂‑N and NO₃‑N at influent, between stages and effluent to assess nitrification/denitrification.  Note that inadequate nitrification may be linked to thick biofilms or insufficient DO .
    
- **Alkalinity, COD and BOD checks** – perform quick field tests or collect samples for laboratory analysis to verify organic removal and buffer capacity.
    
- **Carrier Distribution Survey** – using grid sampling, count carriers per unit volume at several points in each basin to determine whether carriers are evenly distributed or aggregated.
    

  

### **3. Maintenance and Operations Interview**

  

Use these questions to uncover hidden operational issues and capacity constraints.  Encourage operators to provide examples and documentation.

1. **Storm Bypass Frequency** – How often do you bypass the MBBR or discharge partially treated wastewater during heavy rain or storm events?  What is the typical duration and reason for bypass?
    
2. **Aeration Challenges** – Have you experienced frequent blower trips, diffuser clogging or uneven aeration?  How is aeration distribution verified (e.g., periodic bubble tests)?
    
3. **Screen/Sieve Maintenance** – How often do the media retention sieves or screens blind?  Do you have to manually clean them?  Have you experienced media loss through damaged screens?
    
4. **Carrier Management** – Have you had to remove or replace carriers due to excessive wear, fouling or damage?  What proportion of carriers is typically replaced each year?
    
5. **Biofilm Issues** – Do operators routinely monitor biofilm thickness?  Have you observed thick or slimy biofilms that reduce performance?  How is sloughing managed?
    
6. **Process Upsets** – Describe any recent events that caused poor effluent quality, foaming or odor.  Were these linked to influent fluctuations, toxic shocks or equipment failures?  How were they resolved?
    
7. **Nutrient Removal** – Have you struggled to meet ammonia or nitrate limits?  Do you supplement with external carbon sources or adjust alkalinity?  How effective are these strategies?
    
8. **Energy and Chemical Costs** – Are there periods of unusually high energy consumption or chemical usage?  Can you attribute these to specific operating modes or seasonal influent variations?
    
9. **Instrumentation Reliability** – Are your DO, pH and ammonia sensors reliable and regularly calibrated?  Do operators trust sensor readings, or are there discrepancies with grab samples?
    
10. **Staffing and Training** – How many trained operators are available per shift?  Do you feel adequately trained on the specific needs of MBBR systems, including carrier inspection and biofilm management?
    

  

### **4. Reporting and Follow‑Up**

  

After completing the audit, compile the following:

- **Summary of observed red flags and deficiencies** (equipment condition, biofilm health, screen blinding, aeration distribution).
    
- **Comparison of design vs. actual loading and performance** using data from the RFI table.
    
- **Energy and chemical usage evaluation** – identify potential optimizations.
    
- **Recommendations** for corrective actions (e.g., carrier replacement, diffusers cleaning, aeration control upgrades, operational changes, staff training).
    
- **Timeline and priorities** for implementing improvements.
    

  

## **Conclusion**

  

This comprehensive RFI and audit template ensures that all pertinent information is gathered before and during an MBBR audit.  By focusing on design versus actual performance, mechanical integrity, biofilm health and operational practices, the audit will identify inefficiencies, capacity constraints and biological health issues.  Attending to red flags—such as discolored or sinking carriers, thick biofilms, or uneven aeration—can prevent poor effluent quality and extend the life of the system .  Adequate monitoring and maintenance, supported by a thorough RFI, enable data‑driven recommendations to optimize the plant’s performance.
